Welcome to the Soft Matter Group! We explore the fascinating world of soft matter physics through both theoretical and computational approaches. We use the concepts of non-equilibrium statistical mechanics to understand the mechanics properties of soft matter.

I am a theoretical physicist with extensive expertise in soft matter physics, specializing in the dynamics and mechanical properties of glassy systems, granular materials, random elastic networks, and strongly coupled liquids. My work is grounded in advanced theoretical modeling and computational simulations, backed by a deep understanding of statistical physics. I am dedicated to advancing the study of disordered solids, prioritizing quality research that addresses key questions in the field. Beyond my research, I am passionate about education and science outreach, working to make complex scientific ideas accessible to both experts and the broader public. Collaboration and mentorship are central to my approach, and I take pride in guiding graduate students with thoughtful and supportive supervision. In addition to physics, I actively volunteer, and I am multilingual, fluent in Marwadi, Hindi, English, and Bengali. My personal interests extend to philosophy, psychology, art, history, and world cuisines, all of which enrich my professional and personal life.

Research Areas

  • Statistical Physics

    • Equilibrium and non-equilibrium statistical mechanics are two central and interrelated branches of physics that deal with the behavior of systems with many interacting particles. Equilibrium statistical mechanics, based on the laws of thermodynamics, deals with systems that are in a state of thermal balance, meaning that the macroscopic variables such as temperature, pressure and energy are constant in time or fluctuate around their mean values. On the other hand, Non-equilibrium statistical mechanics focuses on the behavior of systems that are not in a state of thermal balance, where the macroscopic variables are changing over time. We focus mainly of non-equilibrium statistical mechanics taking the systems mentioned below as a subject of research. We primary try to understand the mechanical properties of disordered athermal solids such as glass transition, jamming, onset of rigidity, vibrational modes to mention a few. We also try to study pattern formations and fluid instabilities in non-Newtonian fluids, one such example is dusty plasma also known as Yukawa fluids.

  • Granular Materials

    • Granular materials are composed of discrete, macroscopic particles that primarily interact through contact forces, rather than long-range forces. Unlike systems governed by thermal fluctuations, granular materials are athermal and dissipative. They lose energy through friction and inelastic collisions, leading to unique mechanical properties. This non-equilibrium behaviour becomes even more intricate when considering nonspherical particles, such as ellipsoids, which exhibit restricted rotational motion and more complex contact geometries compared to spherical particles (see Fig. 1). Non-spherical particles have anisotropic contact geometries, which influence the transmission of forces through the material and lead to varied stress distributions and force chain structures. The mechanical stability and force chains in such systems depend not only on the packing fraction but also on particle shape, orientation, and contact mechanics, leading to complex force networks that require detailed analysis. Jamming, which refers to the transition from a fluid-like state to a rigid, mechanically stable state, is well-studied in spherical particles, but the jamming behaviour of non-spherical particles remains less explored. While prior studies have explored some aspects of ellipsoidal granular materials, the role of friction in such systems remains insufficiently addressed, mainly due to the complexities of modeling friction in non-spherical geometries. In this group, we try fill this gap.

  • Active matter

    • Active matter, an emerging field of research in soft matter physics, is a prime example of a non-equilibrium system. This field focuses on the behavior of systems composed of self-driven particles that are able to convert energy into motion. The study of active matter includes a wide range of systems, such as swimming microorganisms, synthetic active colloids, and active gels. In these systems, the interactions between the particles and the conversion of energy into motion lead to the emergence of complex and often self-organized behavior, such as pattern formation and collective motion.

  • Complex Plasma (Dusty Plasma)

    • Yukawa liquids (YLs), a type of complex plasma, form when micron-sized dust grains are immersed in an electron-ion plasma, resulting in a strongly coupled system due to the dust grains acquiring high charges (≈ 104e). In such systems, the dust grains interact through a screened Coulomb potential, also known as Yukawa interaction, where the background plasma provides shielding. The small charge-to-mass ratio (< 1), high average potential-to-kinetic energy ratio (> 50) and low temperature (≈ 0.025 eV) lead to the dominance of potential energy over kinetic energy, causing the system to behave in a strongly coupled regime. Depending on the coupling strength, YLs can exist in various states (solid-like, liquid-like, or gas-like), and the liquid phase, in particular, offers a unique opportunity to explore non-equilibrium phenomena and spatiotemporal pattern formation. Due to their mechanical properties, YLs are often described as the plasma state of soft matter exhibiting viscoelastic behavior. This makes them an ideal system to study the effect of long-range interactions and coupling strength on turbulence, and instabilities under non-equilibrium conditions.

  • Random Elastic Networks

    • Random Elastic Networks (RENs) are constructed by considering a set of nodes (representing particles, atoms, or molecules) connected by springs or elastic bonds, forming an irregular network with random connectivity, Z (see Fig. 2) and exhibit dynamic heterogeneity when sheared (see Fig. 3). These networks are particularly useful for modeling materials that lack regular crystalline structures but still exhibit elastic properties when subjected to external forces. The study of RENs provides insights into how local interactions between components (e.g., inter-particle forces or molecular bonds) give rise to macroscopic mechanical properties like elasticity, rigidity, and deformation.

  • Biological physics

    • Biological physics, a multidisciplinary field that combines the principles of physics and biology, is another area of research in soft matter physics. In biological physics, the focus is on understanding the behavior and function of biological systems, such as cells, tissues, and organisms, at the molecular, cellular, and organismal level. The study of biological physics is important for a wide range of applications, including the development of new therapies and treatments for diseases, the design of new materials based on biological principles, and the understanding of evolution and adaptation.
